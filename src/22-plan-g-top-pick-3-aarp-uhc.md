## Plan G — Top Recommendation #3: AARP / UnitedHealthcare Insurance Company (Standard tier)

**The "scale and predictability" pick** — by far the largest Medigap carrier in Michigan and the U.S., with predictable (if not low) rate increases, deep member services infrastructure, and the AARP brand association. The recommendation for buyers who prioritize scale, name recognition, and the ability to switch plans easily within the AARP/UHC ecosystem.

### The One-Line Summary

> **AARP/UHC Standard Plan G costs $182/mo at age 65, holds an A (Excellent) financial strength rating with 83.1% loss ratio and <1.0 NAIC complaint index, is the largest Medigap carrier in Michigan (34.13% market share) and the U.S. (3.8M members, $11B premium), and has averaged 3.3% annual increases from 2017-2022 in Michigan per audited UHC data.** No other Plan G in Michigan offers the scale, name recognition, and member-services infrastructure of AARP/UHC.

### Premium & Tiers

AARP/UHC offers Plan G in Michigan in two tiers with dramatically different premiums:

| Tier | Monthly | Underwriting | Who It Targets |
|------|--------:|--------------|----------------|
| Standard | $182 | Standard health questions | Most 65-year-olds within their Medigap Open Enrollment window (best value) |
| Level 2 | $537 | Higher-risk pool | Higher-risk applicants; same benefits, but priced for the riskier pool |

**Pricing method:** Community (the same premium applies regardless of age — but an "age-related enrollment discount" of up to 45% is offered at age 65 and declines to 0% by age 82, so premiums still rise as the discount phases out)

**Critical distinction:** The "Standard" tier at $182/mo is the right pick for almost all buyers. The "Level 2" tier at $537/mo is **not a "premium" tier** — it's the same Standard plan benefits priced for a higher-risk underwriting pool. It is not a "better" plan in any way. Skip it.

### Why This Is the Top Pick

**1. Largest Medigap carrier in Michigan AND the U.S.**

AARP/UHC holds **127,908 Michigan Medigap members (34.13% of the state's 374,795 total)** — more than 1 in 3 Michigan Medigap enrollees. Nationally, UHC writes **3.8M Medigap members with $11B in direct premiums earned** — more than the next 3 carriers combined. This scale produces: (a) the largest member-services infrastructure (24/7 phone support, online claims tracking, mobile app); (b) the most experience with edge-case claims; (c) the least risk of being closed to new enrollment (the "dead duck pool" scenario is impossible at this scale).

**2. 3.3% historical Michigan rate-increase average (2017-2022, audited).**

UHC is the **only carrier that publishes an audited, multi-year history of Michigan-specific rate increases.** The 2017-2022 average is 3.3% per year — the lowest of any major carrier with published multi-year data. From their 2023 claim substantiation report (audited by Human8): Michigan-specific AARP Medigap Plans averaged 3.3% annual base rate increases from 2017-2022. This is the *gold standard* for rate-increase predictability.

**However — the recent picture is different.** In 2024, UHC took 12.2% nationally (and a similar amount in Michigan). In 2025, 10.3%. In 2026, **17.7% in Michigan specifically** (per the EnrollMedicare Supabase data). The 12-17% range of 2024-2026 is well above the 3.3% historical average. The 3.3% figure is *historical*, not *current* — and the recent trajectory is concerning.

**3. <1.0 NAIC complaint index — better than expected for a carrier its size.**

NAIC complaint data for UnitedHealthcare Insurance Co (NAIC cocode 79413) shows a complaint index **below 1.0** — meaning AARP/UHC Medigap enrollees file *fewer* complaints than expected for a company its size. This is impressive given the 3.8M member count. Absolute complaint volume is high (because the member count is high), but normalized to market share, UHC is better than average.

**4. A (Excellent) financial strength rating, $11B parent block.**

AM Best rates UnitedHealthcare Insurance Co at **A (Excellent) with a Stable outlook** as of August 28, 2025. Note that this is a **one-notch downgrade from A+ (Superior)** in August 2025 — driven by concerns about UHC's Medicare Advantage business, not the Medigap block. The Medigap line remains well-capitalized and is by far the most stable line of business UHC writes.

**5. 83.1% loss ratio — healthy and predictable.**

The 2024 NAIC report shows AARP/UHC at **83.1% loss ratio on $11.077 billion in direct premiums earned**. This is in the healthy 75-90% range and is the most reliable loss ratio in the industry — the 75% group-policy minimum is comfortably exceeded, and the $11B premium block is large enough that single-year variance is minimal. A 83.1% loss ratio on $11B means roughly $9.2B in claims paid and $1.9B in administration/profit — a sustainable balance.

**6. Member services infrastructure — 24/7 support, mobile app, online claims.**

Because of the 3.8M member count, AARP/UHC has the most-developed Medigap member services of any carrier:
- 24/7 phone support
- Online claims tracking
- Mobile app for EOB, ID card, provider lookup
- Nationwide provider network (though Plan G has no network, having a robust provider-search tool is helpful)
- Brand familiarity — most doctors' offices and hospitals know how to bill UHC Medigap

### 20-Year Lifetime Cost

Using AARP/UHC Standard's $182/mo starting premium:

| Timeframe | Premium (3.3%/yr, MI 2017-2022 avg) | Premium (10%/yr, 2024-2025 trend) | Premium (17%/yr, 2026 MI) |
|-----------|-------------------------------------:|----------------------------------:|--------------------------:|
| Age 65 (today) | $182 | $182 | $182 |
| Age 70 (5 years) | $215 | $293 | $397 |
| Age 75 (10 years) | $254 | $471 | $865 |
| Age 80 (15 years) | $299 | $757 | $1,884 |
| Age 85 (20 years) | $353 | $1,217 | $4,103 |
| **20-yr lifetime total** | **$69,700** | **$113,200** | **$194,300** |

The lifetime cost is **highly sensitive to the assumed rate-increase scenario**:

- **If 3.3%/yr continues** (the historical MI 2017-2022 average, the best-case scenario): $69,700 lifetime — *cheapest of the top 3 picks*
- **If 10%/yr continues** (the 2024-2025 trend): $113,200 lifetime — *middle of the top 3*
- **If 17%/yr continues** (the 2026 MI rate, the worst-case scenario): $194,300 lifetime — *most expensive of the top 3*

**The honest assessment:** the 3.3% historical figure is unlikely to continue. The 12-17% range of 2024-2026 reflects claim-cost inflation that the carrier is catching up on. The most realistic projection is somewhere between 8% and 12% per year going forward, which would produce a 20-year lifetime cost of **$95,000–$135,000**.

- ✅ **A (Excellent) financial strength** rating — solid even after the August 2025 downgrade from A+
- ✅ **83.1% loss ratio on $11B premium** — the most reliable loss ratio in the industry
- ✅ **<1.0 NAIC complaint index** — better than expected for a carrier its size
- ✅ **3.3% historical MI rate-increase average (2017-2022, audited)** — proven track record of predictability
- ✅ **Best member services infrastructure** — 24/7 support, mobile app, online claims
- ✅ **AARP brand recognition** — most doctors' offices are familiar with AARP/UHC Medigap
- ✅ **Standard tier at $182/mo is mid-priced** — cheaper than Mutual of Omaha ($220) and most other top picks
- ✅ **Community-rated pricing** — base premium doesn't rise with age (only the inflation adjustment and discount phase-out affect it)

### Cons

- ⚠️ **Recent rate increases are 12-17% in 2024-2026** — well above the historical 3.3% average. The 2026 Michigan rate is the highest in the 2026 batch
- ⚠️ **Class action lawsuit (Sacchi v. AARP, filed February 2026 and reported March 2026)** alleges systematic claim denials based on a "phantom" provider-Medicare-participation requirement. UHC has not admitted liability; the case is in early procedural stages. **Read the user-experiences section for the full picture**
- ⚠️ **Volume of user complaints in 2024-2026 is high** — multiple forum reports of 20-40% annual increases, household-partner rate inconsistencies, and unexpected claim denials. The NAIC complaint index is below 1.0 (good), but the absolute volume of complaints is the highest in the industry due to the 3.8M member count
- ⚠️ **AM Best downgrade from A+ to A (Aug 2025)** — the rating is still "Secure" but the trend is downward, driven by non-Medigap business
- ⚠️ **"Faux community-rated" pricing** — the base premium is community-rated, but the "age-related enrollment discount" declines from ~45% at age 65 to 0% at age 82, so premiums still rise as the discount phases out. This is *not* the same as true community-rated pricing
- ⚠️ **"Standard" vs "Level 2" tier confusion** — the $537/mo Level 2 tier is not a "premium" tier, but the existence of a $537/mo option in the same data set can mislead buyers into thinking there's a "better" plan for more money
- ⚠️ **AARP receives royalty fees tied to branded insurance products** — the AARP-UHC relationship is a brand-licensing deal, not a purely independent endorsement. Public reports show AARP's total royalty revenue is about $1B annually, mostly health-related, but current UHC-specific amounts are not fully disclosed. Some critics argue AARP's endorsement is conflicted

### Best For

A buyer who:
- Values the largest scale and the most member-services infrastructure over the absolute best metrics on each individual axis
- Trusts the AARP brand association and the audited 2017-2022 Michigan history
- Is willing to accept higher recent rate increases (12-17%) in exchange for the predictability of the largest carrier
- Plans to keep the Plan G policy for 10+ years and wants to know the carrier will *definitely* still be there
- Is comfortable with the 2025 AM Best downgrade and the 2026 class action lawsuit
- Wants easy online enrollment and 24/7 phone support
- Lives in a rural area where provider familiarity with the carrier matters (UHC is the most-recognized brand)

### Who Should NOT Pick AARP/UHC

- **The "I want the absolute best quality" buyer** — Mutual of Omaha has A+ Superior (one notch above) and 0.48 complaint index (best in the set)
- **The "I want the lowest rate increases" buyer** — BCBS Michigan at 6.5% recent increases is materially lower
- **The "I want to avoid the recent lawsuit and complaint volume" buyer** — the class action and the user-experience reports are a real signal; BCBS Michigan and Mutual of Omaha don't have these
- **The Michigan-domestic-first buyer** — UHC is a national carrier; BCBS Michigan is MI-DIFS-regulated
- **The buyer on a fixed income who can't absorb 12-17% annual increases** — if 17% continues, premiums reach $865/mo by age 75 and $1,884/mo by age 80. This is a real risk

### Caveats

- **The 3.3% historical rate-increase average is from 2017-2022.** The 2024-2026 rates are 10.3-17.7%, well above the historical average. **Do not assume 3.3% will continue.** The historical figure is useful as a reference point, not a forecast.
- **The 17.7% 2026 Michigan rate is the second-highest in the 2026 batch** (behind only AFLAC's 22.0%). If the 17% trend continues, premiums will roughly double every 4-5 years. This is not sustainable for a fixed-income retiree.
- **The class action lawsuit (Sacchi v. AARP, filed February 2026 and reported March 2026) alleges a "phantom" provider-Medicare-participation requirement** that UHC allegedly uses to deny claims. Read the user-experiences section for first-person reports. UHC has not admitted liability, and the case is in early procedural stages.
- **The complaint index is <1.0, which is good, but the absolute volume of complaints is the highest in the industry** because the member count (3.8M) is the highest. A 0.8 complaint index on 3.8M members is more total complaints than a 1.5 complaint index on 50,000 members.
- **The "Standard" vs "Level 2" tier distinction is not a quality distinction.** Level 2 is the same plan priced for a higher-risk underwriting pool. Do not pay $537/mo thinking you are buying a "better" plan.
- **AARP membership is required** and costs ~$20/year. This is a minor cost but is a prerequisite to enrollment.
- **UHC's August 2025 AM Best downgrade from A+ to A was driven by Medicare Advantage, not Medigap.** The Medigap line remains stable. But the parent company weakness is worth monitoring.
