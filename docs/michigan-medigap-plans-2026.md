## How to Read This Document

- **Plan letter (A–N)** = a standardized plan type. Benefits are *identical* across insurers for the same letter. Only price, pricing method, customer service, and underwriting tier differ.
- **Pricing methods:**
  - *Attained-age* — low when you buy, but goes up each year as you age. Most common.
  - *Community* (also called *no-age-rated*) — same premium for everyone of the same area regardless of age. Premiums may still rise for inflation.
  - *Issue-age* — locked in at the age when you buy. Premiums rise for inflation but not for aging.
- **Plans C and F** are unavailable to anyone who turned 65 on/after Jan 1, 2020. They are listed for completeness.
- **Tier variants** ((Preferred), (Standard), (Tier 1/2/3), (Level 1/2)) are the same plan benefits priced for different risk pools (preferred/standard underwriting tiers, sometimes household-discount tiers).
- **High-deductible plans** (F-high, G-high) require you to pay a large annual plan deductible before coverage begins. Premiums are correspondingly low.
- **All figures are for 2026 Medicare benefit periods.**

## Plan G — Loss Ratio Analysis (2024 Experience)

**Source:** [NAIC 2024 Medicare Supplement Loss Ratio Report](https://content.naic.org/sites/default/files/publication-med-bb-medicare-loss-report.pdf) (released September 10, 2025), reflecting calendar-year 2024 direct premiums earned and direct claims incurred for the parent NAIC-registered entity, countrywide (U.S. and territories), across both individual and group policies.

### What Is a Loss Ratio?

> **Loss Ratio = (Total Claims Paid ÷ Total Premiums Collected) × 100**

It measures the share of every premium dollar that flows back out to policyholders as medical claims. The remainder covers administration, commissions, taxes, and profit. A higher loss ratio means policyholders are getting more claims paid relative to what they paid in — i.e., more value per premium dollar.

### Federal Minimum Loss Ratio Standards

The Medicare Catastrophic Coverage Act of 1988 (and subsequent NAIC regulations) requires Medigap insurers to maintain loss ratios at or above:

| Policy Type | Minimum Loss Ratio |
|-------------|-------------------:|
| Group policies (AARP-style, employer-group, association) | **75%** |
| Individual policies (sold direct to consumer) | **65%** |

If an insurer falls below the threshold, state insurance commissioners can require premium refunds to policyholders or order a rate adjustment. Note: in Michigan, AARP/UnitedHealthcare is sold as a *group* policy (because only AARP members can buy it), so it must meet the 75% threshold rather than the 65% individual one.

1. **Cheap premium ≠ best value.** Michigan Farm Bureau ($113/mo) is the cheapest Plan G in Michigan but has no public loss ratio. BCBS Michigan ($191/mo) is 69% more expensive, but their 86.3% loss ratio means ~86 cents of every premium dollar goes to claims. That's a *higher* payout ratio than the industry average.
2. **High loss ratios often precede rate hikes.** Carriers above 100% (American Benefit Life, Humana Achieve) are losing money on the block and are likely to file for premium increases in 2026–2027. Your "cheap" premium may not stay cheap.
3. **AARP/UHC is the gold standard for stability.** $11 billion in premiums, 83.1% loss ratio, 3.8 million covered lives. The AARP name is the group wrapper; the actual underwriter is UnitedHealthcare Insurance Co (CT). The 75% group minimum is well exceeded, and UHC has the financial scale to keep the block stable.
4. **Michigan-domestic carriers (Priority Health, BCBS MI, HAP/McLaren, Michigan Farm Bureau) all cluster around 83–86% loss ratios**, which is healthy and close to the national 84.5% benchmark.
5. **Aetna and Cigna are under margin pressure, but not equally.** Aetna/Continental Life's 86.5% loss ratio is healthy-to-high, while Cigna/HealthSpring's 99.3% is close to breakeven. Both have recent rate-filing activity, but Cigna's loss ratio is the clearer warning sign.
6. **For long-term price stability, look for:** Loss ratio between 75% and 90%, parent company with >$100M in Medigap premium (diversified block), and a recent (2024–2025) rate increase in Michigan of less than 8%. Best fits: BCBS Michigan, Priority Health, AARP/UHC, State Farm, USAA.

- **Loss ratios are countrywide, not Michigan-specific.** State-level experience can vary. Michigan-specific Medigap loss ratios are not publicly published, but the national figures are the best available proxy.

- **Mutual of Omaha entities are reported separately.** Mutual of Omaha has 5 separate NAIC entities (Mutual of Omaha, Omaha Ins Co, United of Omaha, United World Life, Omaha Supplemental) with loss ratios ranging from 77% to 100%. The NAIC complaint index measures how many complaints a carrier receives relative to its market share — **1.0 = expected for a company its size**, below 1.0 = fewer complaints, above 1.0 = more complaints. **Lower is better.** Values marked "estimated" are directional placeholders from secondary research, not verified NAIC company-level figures.

### Why This Matters for Choosing a Plan G

Loss ratio tells you how much of your premium dollar is paid out as claims. The complaint index tells you how painful the experience is *while you're filing those claims*. A carrier with a great loss ratio but bad complaint index is one where you might have to call them five times to get a claim paid. A carrier with mediocre loss ratio but great complaint index is one where things just work.

## Plan G — Historical Rate Increase Analysis (2017–2026)

A cheap premium today doesn't mean a cheap premium tomorrow. Insurers file annual rate increases with the state insurance department, and a 6% increase on a $200/mo policy adds ~$144/year in extra premium. ### Important Caveats (Restated)

- **Filing ≠ effective date.** Insurers file with the state months in advance. The "2026" numbers in the table above are based on filings made in early 2026 and may not yet be effective.
- **The carriers that haven't filed a 2026 MI rate increase may still file one later in 2026.** The EnrollMedicare database only knows what's been filed; missing data ≠ "no increase planned."
- **Tier variants** (Preferred, Standard, Substandard, Tier 1/2/3) often have different rate-increase schedules. Where CSG Actuarial reports one number for a carrier, that number is typically a Plan-G-specific rate for the carrier's primary tier.
- **The 2017-2022 UHC MI data is the only carrier-specific multi-year audit publicly available.** All other "histories" are reconstructed from a patchwork of state filings.
- **Loss ratios and rate increases are not the same thing.** A carrier with 75% loss ratio and 5% rate increase is doing fine; a carrier with 95% loss ratio and 5% rate increase is falling further behind. The CSG Actuarial Telos data shows the *whole* carrier's experience; Michigan block experience may differ.

3. **Attained-age aging.** The extra you pay each year simply for getting older:

   AARP/UnitedHealthcare plan (its age discount declines with age), and **+0%** for the

A carrier's **annual growth = carrier trend + attained-age aging** (inflation is already inside the trend
figure — adding it again would double-count). Because the filed increases *already embed* inflation and

## Plan G — Financial Strength, Market Share & Long-Term Cost

This section completes the "carrier quality" picture by adding **AM Best financial strength ratings** and **Michigan Medigap market share** data to the loss ratio, complaint index, and rate-increase analyses in the previous sections. Combined, these four metrics answer the question every new Plan G enrollee should ask: *"Will this carrier be around in 20 years, and will it have been able to keep my premium reasonable?"*

### Why Financial Strength Matters

Plan G has no network — it pays whatever Medicare doesn't, on every claim. The only thing that determines whether a Plan G enrollee gets a clean claim paid is the **financial stability of the carrier**. A carrier with:

- A weak AM Best rating (B+ or lower) is at higher risk of insolvency, regulatory action, or being absorbed by a stronger carrier
- A negative outlook from AM Best is on watch for downgrade
- A small or shrinking Michigan Medigap block is at risk of being closed to new enrollment
- A history of double-digit rate increases is at risk of future double-digit increases (high loss ratios + high utilization = insurance industry math doesn't lie)

The four-metric picture: **AM Best** (will they pay claims?) + **Loss ratio** (are they keeping up with costs?) + **Complaint index** (do they pay claims cleanly?) + **Rate increase** (how fast is my premium growing?) + **Market share** (do they have scale?).

### Important Caveats

- **AM Best ratings are point-in-time.** A rating can change at any time. Check AM Best's website for the current rating before purchasing.
- **Outlook ≠ rating action.** A Negative outlook means AM Best is *considering* a downgrade; it is not a downgrade. Most Negative-outlook ratings are not actually downgraded.
- **Michigan market share is reported anonymously in UHC's report.** The "Carrier #2" with 27.47% share is almost certainly BCBS Michigan based on (1) legacy 50%+ market share before UHC's AARP-driven growth, (2) BCBS MI's overall 36.65% of Michigan health insurance, (3) BCBS MI being a Michigan-only BCBS licensee. But the UHC report doesn't confirm it directly.
- **Mutual of Omaha and Humana market share figures are estimates** based on national rankings and the Michigan market structure, not direct UHC report confirmation.
- **A high loss ratio with a recent large rate increase can be misleading.** A 99% loss ratio this year might be paired with a 22% rate increase that brings next year's loss ratio back to 75%. Look at the 2-3 year trend, not a single year.
- **The complaint index uses the same scale (0 = perfect, 1 = industry average, 2+ = twice as many complaints as average).** It is normalized by premium volume, so a small carrier with a few complaints can have a high index that doesn't reflect real-world claims experience.
- **AM Best does not rate every Medigap-issuing entity.** HAP, McLaren, and Everence (fraternal benefit society) are not AM Best rated. Their absence is structural, not a quality flag — but you have less public data to evaluate them.

### Class Action Lawsuit — Sacchi v. AARP, et al. (filed February 2026; reported March 2026)

A proposed nationwide class action was filed in **U.S. District Court for the District of New Jersey** in February 2026 by plaintiff John Sacchi and was reported by class-action news outlets in March 2026. The complaint alleges:

> *"For decades, [UnitedHealthcare] has violated the subject promises to pay for medically necessary healthcare not paid by Medicare. [UHC] intends to continue systematically denying such claims by citing a phantom, non-existent condition that appears nowhere in the policies' subject Certificate of Insurance."*

The "phantom condition" allegedly used as a denial basis: **the healthcare provider must "participate in or accept Medicare"** for the claim to be covered. Plaintiffs argue this requirement is **nowhere in the Certificate of Insurance** but is being invoked to deny claims.

**Lead plaintiff's case:** John Sacchi, a senior citizen and stroke survivor, underwent medically necessary surgery to remove cancerous tissue from his eyelid and a follow-up corrective procedure. After being told by an AARP representative on a recorded call that the surgery would be covered, UHC denied the claim — leaving him with nearly **$8,000 in unpaid medical bills**.

**Class sought:** All individuals who, since 2014, were AARP members and policyholders of an AARP Medicare Supplement plan from UnitedHealthcare and received one or more denials of reimbursement for medically necessary healthcare on the basis that the provider did not participate in or accept Medicare.

**AARP's alleged role:** The complaint alleges AARP receives royalty fees from UHC for the use of the AARP brand on these Medigap plans, making AARP a knowing participant in the alleged scheme. Public reports show AARP's total annual royalty revenue is about $1 billion, mostly health-related, but current UHC-specific amounts are not fully disclosed and AARP disputes critics' characterization of the relationship.

**Status as of June 2026:** Case is in early procedural stages. No class certification yet. UHC has not publicly admitted liability.

**Source:** [classaction.org](https://www.classaction.org/news/class-action-lawsuit-says-aarp-unitedhealthcare-fraudulently-deny-medicare-supplement-claims), [topclassactions.com](https://topclassactions.com/lawsuit-settlements/lawsuit-news/aarp-unitedhealthcare-class-action-alleges-wrongful-medicare-supplement-claim-denials/), [classactionu.org](https://classactionu.org/our-news/aarp-and-unitedhealthcare-hit-with-class-action-lawsuit-over-alleged-medicare-supplement-fraud/)

### Important Caveats

- **The most-membership carriers get the most complaints.** UHC has 3.8M members. AFLAC has tens of thousands. Comparing absolute complaint counts between them is unfair — that's why the NAIC complaint *index* is more useful.
- **Some complaints are about Medicare Advantage, not Medigap.** I tried to filter for Medigap-specific complaints. The line between "AARP/UHC Medigap" and "AARP/UHC Medicare Advantage" is blurred in some forum posts.
- **Forum users may not represent the typical customer.** People who post on forums are more likely to have had a strong experience (positive or negative) than the average member.
- **Michigan-specific data is limited** because Michigan doesn't have a high-traffic dedicated Medigap forum. Most of the user-experience data here is from AARP Community (which has Michigan posters) and other state-based sources.
- **The class action lawsuit is an allegation, not a proven fact.** UHC has not admitted liability, and the case is in early stages. But the existence of the lawsuit (and the 26-page detail in the complaint) is itself a signal that something unusual may be happening with AARP/UHC Medigap claims.

## Medigap Switching Rights in Michigan

**This is the most important section of this document to read before you sign up for a plan.** Choosing the wrong Medigap carrier can lock you into 5, 10, or 20 years of premium increases with no easy way out.

### The Core Rule: Federal 6-Month Open Enrollment Period

When you first enroll in **Medicare Part B** (typically at age 65), you have a **6-month Medigap Open Enrollment Period (OEP)**. During this window:

- **No medical underwriting.** Insurers must accept you regardless of your health.
- **No rate-ups** for pre-existing conditions.
- **Limited pre-existing-condition waiting-period risk.** If you have sufficient prior creditable coverage, the insurer generally cannot make you wait for pre-existing-condition coverage; without creditable coverage, a waiting period may still apply.
- You can buy **any Medigap plan** sold in Michigan.

**After this 6-month window closes**, in most states (including Michigan), insurers can:
- **Deny your application** based on health
- **Charge you a higher premium** (rate-up)
- **Impose a 6-month waiting period** for pre-existing conditions

This is why insurance brokers push you to "decide during your OEP." Once it closes, you may be stuck with your current carrier for life.

### Does Michigan Have a Medigap Birthday Rule?

**❌ No. Michigan does not have a Medigap birthday rule.**

As of June 2026, only the following states have a Medigap birthday rule (each with different terms):

| State | Birthday Rule | Window | Can Upgrade Plans? |
|---|---|---|---|
| California | ✅ | 60 days after birthday | No (equal or lesser) |
| Oregon | ✅ | 60 days after birthday | No (equal or lesser) |
| Idaho | ✅ | 63 days after birthday | No (equal or lesser) |
| Illinois | ✅ | 45 days before + 30 days after birthday | No (equal or lesser) |
| Nevada | ✅ | 60 days after birthday | No (equal or lesser) |
| Louisiana | ✅ | 30 days before + 63 days after policy anniversary | No (equal or lesser) |
| Missouri | ✅ | 30 days before + 30 days after policy anniversary | No (equal or lesser) |
| Maryland | ✅ | 30 days after birthday | No (equal or lesser) |
| Rhode Island | ✅ | 30 days after birthday | No (equal or lesser) |
| New York | ✅ | Year-round | ✅ Yes (any plan) |
| Connecticut | ✅ | Year-round | ✅ Yes (any plan) |
| Massachusetts | ✅ | Year-round | ✅ Yes (any plan) |
| Maine | ✅ | Year-round | ✅ Yes (any plan) |
| Vermont | ✅ | Year-round | ✅ Yes (any plan) |

**Michigan's status:** No birthday rule, no annual Medigap OEP, no year-round guaranteed issue. After the initial 6-month OEP closes, **you must pass medical underwriting to switch to a new Medigap carrier**.

### Pending Michigan Legislation

**Senate Bill 1143 of 2024** (introduced November 26, 2024) would create a Medigap switching right in Michigan. Separate 2025 proposals have also been discussed. As of June 2026, no Michigan Medigap birthday-rule bill has been enacted.

**If passed**, the bill would likely:
- Allow Michigan Medigap enrollees to switch to the **same plan letter** (Plan G → Plan G, etc.) with a different carrier
- Apply only during a limited window around the policyholder's birthday
- Prohibit medical underwriting during the window
- **Not** allow plan upgrades (Plan N → Plan G)
- Possibly also expand under-65 access and require more favorable premiums for disabled enrollees

**Until a bill passes, plan as though the birthday rule won't arrive in time for your next enrollment decision.** Sources: [Michigan Legislature SB 1143 of 2024](https://www.legislature.mi.gov/Bills/Bill?ObjectName=2024-SB-1143), [Eligry Medicare Solutions](https://eligry.com/michigan-medicare-supplement-underwriting-rules-2026-eligry/), [Senior65 Ultimate Guide](https://www.senior65.com/medicare/article/medigap-birthday-rule-ultimate-state-guide).

### What You Can Still Do After Your OEP Closes

You have **limited options** for switching Medigap plans outside the OEP in Michigan:

**1. Federal Guaranteed Issue Rights (Trial Rights)**

You can buy certain Medigap plans without underwriting in these specific situations:

- **You joined a Medicare Advantage (MA) plan when first eligible at 65, and you want to switch back to Original Medicare + Medigap within 12 months.** This is the "MA trial right" — but you can only use it once.
- **You had Medigap, then joined MA for the first time, then want to switch back to Medigap within 12 months.**
- **Your Medigap insurer goes bankrupt or ends your policy.**
- **You move out of your Medigap insurer's service area** (less relevant for Medigap since it has no networks, but applies to Medicare SELECT plans).
- **You lose employer-sponsored health coverage** (group health plan that supplemented Medicare).

In these cases, you generally have **63 days** from the triggering event to buy certain Medigap plans without underwriting. Michigan DIFS lists standardized Plans **A, B, C, F, high-deductible F, K, or L** for guaranteed-issue rights, with MACRA reassignments for people newly eligible for Medicare on or after Jan 1, 2020: **C → D, F → G, and high-deductible F → high-deductible G**. **Plan N is not included** in the standard federal/Michigan guaranteed-issue set.

**2. Michigan-Specific Under-65 Rights**

Michigan DIFS says that if you are under age 65 and on Medicare, your Medicare Supplement choices are generally limited to **Plan A or Plan C**, from a limited number of insurers required to offer those plans to under-65 beneficiaries. For people newly eligible for Medicare on or after Jan 1, 2020, **Plan C is reassigned as Plan D** because MACRA prohibits newly eligible beneficiaries from buying Medigap coverage for the Part B deductible. Higher premiums may be allowed. Verify current under-65 availability directly with Michigan DIFS or MMAP before relying on this right.

**3. Medical Underwriting (the default path)**

This is how most Michigan Medigap switches happen. The new carrier reviews:
- Your health history (typically questions about the past 2-5 years)
- Your prescription medications
- Tobacco use
- Height/weight
- Any pending surgeries or hospitalizations
- Common conditions: heart disease, cancer (within 5 years), diabetes, COPD, kidney disease, etc.

You may be:
- **Approved at standard rates** — same as anyone else your age
- **Approved with a rate-up** — 10-50% higher than standard
- **Approved with a pre-existing condition waiting period** — up to 6 months (some carriers waive this)
- **Declined** — no offer at all

**Tip:** Some carriers (like BCBS Michigan with their "Blue to Blue" program) allow internal switches between their own Medigap plans with relaxed underwriting. If you have a BCBS Michigan Medigap policy, call them at 877-469-2583 to ask about internal switches before trying to switch carriers.

### The Trap: Closed-Block Carriers

If your carrier **stops accepting new Medigap enrollees** (Manhattan Life, ACE/Chubb, and Allstate have all done this in 2025), your block becomes a "closed block." Without new young/healthy members coming in, the average age of the block rises, claims rise, and the carrier files for large rate increases. **One Chubb broker reported 80 customers in Illinois hit with 45% increases** in a single filing.

If you are currently enrolled with a closed-block carrier in Michigan, your options are:
1. **Apply for medical underwriting elsewhere now**, while you may still be in decent health
2. **Wait for Michigan's birthday rule to pass** (uncertain timeline)
3. **Stay put and accept the rate increases** (often the worst option)

### What to Do Right Now

**If you are still in your 6-month Medigap OEP (turning 65, just enrolled in Part B):**

1. **Don't pick based on premium alone.** Pick based on:
   - 3-5 year rate-increase history in Michigan
   - NAIC complaint index
   - Loss ratio
   - Whether the carrier has a history of closing blocks
2. **Compare at least 3 carriers' Plan G** (this document covers all 41).
3. **Verify the carrier is committed to the Michigan Medigap market** for the long term.

**If you are outside your OEP and unhappy with your current carrier:**

1. **Get quotes from 3-5 alternative carriers** based on your specific age, ZIP, and health.
2. **Apply to the alternative carrier** — they will underwrite you. If approved, you have 30 days to "free-look" the new policy and cancel the old one.
3. **Do not cancel your current policy** until the new one is approved and in force.
4. **Be aware** that switching means you lose any rate-stability history you have with your current carrier.

**If you are approaching age 65 and considering Medicare Advantage instead of Medigap:**

1. **Understand** that once you choose MA, you can usually only get a Medigap policy with medical underwriting after the first 12 months. (Trial right exception.)
2. **You can change from MA back to Medigap** during the 12-month trial right, but you can only use this once.

### What MMAP and DIFS Will Not Do

MMAP and DIFS counselors:
- ✅ Can explain Medigap rules and how they apply to you
- ✅ Can help you understand rate-increase letters
- ✅ Can file complaints on your behalf with carriers
- ❌ **Cannot** recommend specific carriers or plans (they are unbiased)
- ❌ **Cannot** sell you insurance (they don't take commissions)
- ❌ **Cannot** negotiate your premium

For carrier-specific advice, you'll need a **licensed insurance agent** (who is paid on commission by the carrier) or a **fee-based Medicare advisor** (who is paid by you).

### Important Caveats

- **The "Blue to Blue" internal switch program is BCBS-Michigan-specific** and can change at any time. Always verify current terms with BCBS Michigan directly.
- **Federal guaranteed issue rights have strict 63-day windows.** Miss the window and you lose the right.
- **Medical underwriting decisions are made by each individual carrier** and can vary significantly. A decline from one carrier doesn't mean another will decline.
- **Smoking, weight, and certain health conditions** can each trigger rate-ups or declines. Be honest on the application — material misrepresentation gives the carrier a basis to rescind your policy later.
- **Pending Michigan legislation is not a guarantee.** S.B.1143 could pass, could pass in modified form, or could die in committee. Don't make a financial decision based on a "maybe."

## Things to Verify Before You Sign

1. **Pricing method** — most are attained-age, so premiums will rise each year. Ask for a projection at age 70, 75, and 80.
2. **Household discount** — many carriers (AARP/UHC, BCBS Michigan, Priority Health, Mutual of Omaha, etc.) offer 5–12% off if another household member also has a policy with them.
3. **Rate stability history** — a cheap premium today may not be cheap in 5 years. Look up the carrier's rate increase history in Michigan.
4. **Underwriting tier** — outside your 6-month Medigap Open Enrollment window, insurers can require medical underwriting and decline you. Buy during the window if at all possible.
5. **Tier variants** — (Preferred), (Standard), (Tier 1/2/3) usually reflect underwriting class or household-discount status. Verify which tier you actually qualify for before quoting.
6. **Substandard tier** — carriers list substandard-tier policies (e.g., Bankers Life (Substandard)) for higher-risk applicants; standard applicants pay less.
7. **No standard Medigap networks** — if Medicare approves the service, the Medigap policy generally pays its standardized share regardless of insurer. Provider billing/assignment edge cases can still matter, so verify unusual services before treatment.
