Choosing a Medicare Plan G Carrier in Michigan

Every insurer's Plan G covers exactly the same things by law — so the real decision is which company to trust to keep it affordable and pay your claims for the next 20 years. This guide walks through what we compared, the numbers for every carrier, and our three top picks.

A cheap premium is the easiest number to see and the most misleading one to choose on. To compare carriers fairly, we gathered five pieces of public data on each company. Here's what each one is, where it comes from, and why it matters when you're picking a plan you may keep for decades.

$

Monthly Premium

Source: Medicare.gov Plan Finder, 2026

What you pay each month, on top of your standard Part B premium. For this profile, Plan G premiums range from $113 to $537 a month — for identical coverage. The lowest price is tempting, but it's only the starting price.

Good sign: a low-to-mid premium from a carrier that also scores well on the measures below.
Warning sign: the very cheapest price from a carrier with no track record — it rarely stays cheapest.
%

Loss Ratio

Source: NAIC 2024 Medicare Supplement Loss Ratio Report

The share of every premium dollar the insurer pays back out as medical claims. An 86% loss ratio means 86¢ of each dollar goes to claims and 14¢ to overhead and profit. A healthy range is roughly 75–90%. Too low can mean you're being overcharged; above 95–100% is the real red flag — the carrier is barely breaking even and will almost certainly raise rates to catch up.

Good sign: 75–90% — fair pricing that's keeping pace with claims.
Warning sign: over 95% (rate hikes coming) or under 72% (possibly overpriced).
★

Complaint Index

Source: National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC)

How often a company's members complain, adjusted for its size. 1.0 is exactly average; below 1.0 means fewer complaints than expected, above 1.0 means more. This is the number that tells you how painful it will be to actually get a claim paid — whether the phone gets answered and the bill gets handled without five callbacks. The best carriers here sit near 0.5; the worst are above 3.0.

Good sign: well below 1.0 — claims tend to "just work."
Warning sign: 1.5 or higher — expect friction and follow-up calls.
↑

Rate-Increase History

Source: Michigan SERFF filings & CSG Actuarial, 2024–2026

How fast a carrier has been raising premiums. This matters more than today's price. Plan G premiums rise every year, and the difference between a carrier averaging 6% and one averaging 15% is enormous over time: a $190 premium growing 6% a year is about $340 in ten years — at 15% a year it's about $770. A steady, single-digit history is worth paying a little more up front.

Good sign: a steady mid-single-digit history (e.g., 6–8% a year).
Warning sign: double-digit jumps, or a "lumpy" pattern with sudden 20%+ spikes.
◆

Financial Strength & Size

Source: AM Best ratings & NAIC market-share data

AM Best grades an insurer's ability to pay claims on a scale from A++ (Superior) down. A- or better is considered "Secure." We also look at how many Michigan members a carrier has: a large, active book of business is far less likely to be closed off and left to spiral into steep "dead-end" rate increases. For a policy you'll hold for 20 years, you want a financially strong carrier that's clearly committed to the Michigan market.

Good sign: AM Best A- or higher, with a large, active Michigan membership.
Warning sign: a B++ rating, or a tiny book of business that could be closed.
The bottom line for tab 2 and 3: No single number decides it. The carriers we recommend are the ones that score well across all five — a fair price, a healthy loss ratio, few complaints, a calm rate history, and the financial strength to still be here in 20 years.

How these are projected

Each carrier grows at its own estimated annual rate, built from three parts (full method in the 18b section of the printable report):

  • Inflation baseline — 3%/yr: general medical-cost inflation, shared by every carrier.
  • Carrier trend: each carrier's own historical filed increases (the "Rate increases by year" on tab 2), above or below that 3% baseline. A history of steep hikes adds to growth; a calm history subtracts.
  • Attained-age aging — 2.5%/yr: the extra you pay each year simply for getting older. Applied to attained-age plans, reduced for community-rated AARP/UHC, and zero for issue-age Everence (its price is locked to your age at purchase).

These are illustrative, not quotes. Real increases are lumpy and unknowable; a carrier with little or no filing history is grown at the Plan G industry average. Carriers whose trend rests on a single filing are marked † in the legend — their long-run rank is low-confidence (a one-off cut or spike can make them look artificially calm or harsh). Ranged premiums use the lowest tier. Don't read three-decimal precision into a 20-year forecast — read the shape: which carriers stay calm and which compound fastest.
